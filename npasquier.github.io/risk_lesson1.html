I. The concept of risk aversion

In words, an agent is risk averse when it prefers to get a certain amount of money, say 18€, rather than to play a lottery that provides the same amount of money but only in expectation, say lottery B (50%*24 + 50%*12 = 18€).

Formally, we say the agent holds a concave utility function where the expected utility (or benefit) provided by the certain amount of money is greater than the expected utility provided by any lottery giving the same amount of money in expectation. The figure below illustrates this sentence.

An easy way to remember that a utility (or generally a function) is concave is to draw a line between any two points belonging to the utility. If the drawn line is below the utility, then the utility is concave (several examples are drawn on the next figure).
